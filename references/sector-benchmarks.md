# Sector Benchmarks — Typical Financial Profiles

Use these as reference ranges when evaluating a company. A company significantly below these benchmarks without explanation warrants scrutiny. A company claiming to be significantly above them warrants verification.

---

## Trucking / Land Freight

| Metric | Weak | Average | Strong |
|--------|------|---------|--------|
| Gross margin | <10% | 10–18% | >18% |
| EBITDA margin | <10% | 15–25% | >25% |
| Net margin | <2% | 3–7% | >7% |
| FCF margin | <0% | 2–6% | >6% |
| ROE | <5% | 8–15% | >15% |
| Debt/EBITDA | >4× | 2–3× | <2× |
| Interest coverage | <2× | 3–5× | >5× |

**Key characteristic**: Asset-heavy, capital-intensive. Fleet depreciation is real economic cost — EBITDA is a poor proxy for earnings power. Always use FCF. Watch for lease accounting that hides debt.

**Healthy pattern**: Strong operating CF > capex, with capex declining as fleet matures. FCF turns positive as the growth phase ends.

**Value trap pattern**: Perpetual fleet expansion funded by debt/equity, FCF never positive, interest coverage declining, margins compressing as older fleet requires more maintenance.

## Manufacturing (General)

| Metric | Weak | Average | Strong |
|--------|------|---------|--------|
| Gross margin | <15% | 20–35% | >35% |
| EBITDA margin | <8% | 12–20% | >20% |
| Net margin | <3% | 5–10% | >10% |
| FCF margin | <0% | 3–8% | >8% |
| ROE | <8% | 12–20% | >20% |
| Capex/Revenue | >15% | 5–10% | <5% |

**Key characteristic**: Watch for inventory build-up and receivables growth relative to revenue — these are early warning signs of demand weakness or collection problems.

---

## Consumer Goods / Retail

| Metric | Weak | Average | Strong |
|--------|------|---------|--------|
| Gross margin | <20% | 25–45% | >45% |
| EBITDA margin | <5% | 8–15% | >15% |
| Net margin | <2% | 4–10% | >10% |
| FCF margin | <0% | 3–8% | >8% |
| Inventory turns | <3× | 5–8× | >8× |

**Key characteristic**: Working capital management matters — days inventory outstanding (DIO) and days sales outstanding (DSO) are critical. Fast-moving consumer goods companies with high DSO may have channel stuffing problems.

---

## Technology / Software

| Metric | Weak | Average | Strong |
|--------|------|---------|--------|
| Gross margin | <40% | 55–75% | >75% |
| EBITDA margin | <0% | 10–25% | >25% |
| Net margin | <0% | 5–20% | >20% |
| FCF margin | <0% | 8–20% | >20% |
| Revenue growth | <10% | 15–30% | >30% |

**Key characteristic**: In early-stage tech, FCF may legitimately be negative due to R&D investment — but verify that losses are narrowing, not widening, as revenue grows. Watch for revenue recognition: when does the company book revenue? (Upfront vs. ratable vs. milestone-based)

---

## Property / Real Estate

| Metric | Weak | Average | Strong |
|--------|------|---------|--------|
| Gross margin | <20% | 25–40% | >40% |
| Net margin | <5% | 10–20% | >20% |
| Debt/Equity | >2× | 0.5–1× | <0.5× |
| Interest coverage | <2× | 3–5× | >5× |

**Key characteristic**: Book value matters more here than in most sectors — land and property assets are more liquid than industrial equipment. But check for land that has been revalued upward without cash flow support.

## Banking / Financial Services

Standard industrial metrics do not apply. For banks, use:
- Return on Assets (ROA): <0.8% weak, 1–1.5% average, >1.5% strong
- Return on Equity (ROE): <8% weak, 12–18% average, >18% strong
- Non-Performing Loan (NPL) ratio: >5% concerning, <2% healthy
- Capital Adequacy Ratio (CAR): regulatory minimum varies by jurisdiction; well-capitalized is typically >13–15%
- Net Interest Margin (NIM): varies by market; compression is a warning sign

---

## Mining / Resources

Highly cyclical — all metrics depend heavily on commodity prices. For due diligence, focus on:
- All-in sustaining cost (AISC) vs. spot commodity price — what's the margin?
- Reserve life — how many years of proven reserves remain?
- Debt structure — is it tied to commodity price covenants?
- Owner track record — do they have a history of writing off assets?

---

## How to use these benchmarks

1. Identify the company's primary sector
2. Pull 3–5 years of the relevant metrics
3. Compare to the benchmark ranges
4. For any metric that is persistently "Weak," ask: is there a structural explanation, or is something wrong?

A company can be in the "Weak" zone on one or two metrics and still be investable if the explanation is credible (e.g., a trucking company with thin margins but exceptional contract quality and locked-in revenue). The concern arises when multiple metrics are simultaneously weak with no clear trajectory of improvement.
