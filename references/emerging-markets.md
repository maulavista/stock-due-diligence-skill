# Emerging Markets Due Diligence — Additional Red Flags

## Structural risks common to EM small-caps

### Ownership opacity
- Beneficial ownership is often concealed behind nominee directors and offshore holding entities
- "Public float" can be illusory — the controlling family may own 80%+ through proxies not required to be disclosed
- Cross-shareholding between group companies makes it nearly impossible to trace ultimate beneficial ownership
- Check: does the company itself acknowledge who the "ultimate controlling shareholder" is in its annual report?

### Capital market behavior patterns
- **IPO pump**: Stock is listed, insiders distribute at peak, stock collapses back to near-zero over 2–3 years. Very common in Indonesia, Malaysia, Vietnam.
- **Placement-dilution cycle**: Company raises capital through private placements every 12–18 months at modest discounts; insiders participate; minority shareholders are perpetually diluted.
- **Price manipulation via low float**: With 40–50% in controlling hands and thin public float, controlling shareholders can move the price with small volumes. Watch for volume spikes with no news.
- **FCA/watchlist placement**: In Indonesia, check if the stock has been placed on the Papan Pemantauan Khusus (FCA board). This indicates the exchange itself has flagged governance or trading abnormalities.

### Related-party transaction patterns
The most common forms of value extraction via RPTs:
1. **Captive customer**: Listed company's main customer is a private affiliate. Revenue is real but pricing may be below-market; the margin stays in the private entity.
2. **Captive supplier**: Listed company buys inputs from a private affiliate at above-market prices. Profits are transferred out before reaching minority shareholders.
3. **Asset sale/leaseback**: Private entity sells assets to the listed company at inflated prices; listed company bears depreciation; private entity pockets the gain.
4. **Management fees**: Listed company pays management/service/consulting fees to a private holding company controlled by the same owner. These appear in SG&A and are rarely broken out.
5. **Captive financing**: Listed company borrows from an affiliated private lender at above-market rates. Interest expense transfers value to the private side.

### Auditor risk
- Small audit firms (non-Big 4) in EM markets may lack independence, technical capacity, or the incentive to challenge a controlling shareholder
- Frequent auditor changes are a red flag — ask why
- Qualified audit opinions are rare; an unqualified opinion from a small firm provides limited assurance
- Look for audit commentary on going concern, related-party transactions, and internal control weaknesses

### Regulatory and legal context by market

**Indonesia (IDX / OJK)**
- POJK (OJK regulation) requires disclosure of RPTs >0.5% of equity, but enforcement against small-caps is minimal
- Minority shareholders can theoretically sue for fiduciary breach, but courts are slow and outcomes uncertain
- IDX's FCA board placement is a soft warning with no hard enforcement consequences
- 10% private placement rule: issuances up to 10% of outstanding shares require only board (not shareholder) approval — exploited routinely

**Malaysia (Bursa)**
- Stricter RPT rules: transactions >1% of NTA require shareholder approval
- More active enforcement by SC (Securities Commission) than Indonesia
- Still significant family-controlled conglomerate risk in small-caps

**Vietnam (HoSE/HNX)**
- Very opaque ownership structures; beneficial ownership disclosure is weak
- State-owned enterprise (SOE) stakes create political risk
- Accounting standards lag IFRS significantly; treat financial statements with extra skepticism

**Philippines (PSE)**
- Conglomerate structures (Ayala, SM, JG Summit, etc.) dominate; minority investors in sub-listed entities face similar extraction risk
- RPT rules exist but related-party transactions are extremely common and often not commercially disadvantageous

**Thailand (SET)**
- Family-controlled conglomerates are the norm
- Better disclosure than Indonesia/Vietnam but still significant governance risk in mid/small-caps
- Watch for inter-company loans that appear on balance sheet as "loans to related parties"

## Scoring adjustments for EM context

When applying the governance scoring from the main skill, add 1 to the red flag count if:
- The stock is listed in Indonesia, Vietnam, or Philippines (baseline governance discount)
- The auditor is not a Big 4 firm or a recognized second-tier firm
- The company has never paid a dividend despite 5+ years of profitability

This reflects the structural enforcement gap in these markets relative to developed market benchmarks.

## What "arm's-length" means in practice

A related-party transaction is theoretically acceptable if it is conducted "at arm's length" — meaning the price and terms are equivalent to what would be negotiated with an unrelated third party. In practice, verifying this is difficult for an outside investor. Questions to ask:

1. Is the pricing explicitly compared to market rates in the disclosure?
2. Are there third-party benchmarks cited (independent valuations, market rate surveys)?
3. Does the auditor specifically comment on the fairness of the RPT pricing?
4. Would the company be able to find a third-party provider of the same service at the same price?

If any of these are absent, treat the RPT as a potential value transfer and discount accordingly.
